## §1 — Petition (filed 2023-02-10)
The Claimant, Jordan Vale, brings this action against the Respondent, Robin Marsh,
seeking reparation for moral damages allegedly arising from defamatory statements.
The Claimant requests the Respondent be ordered to pay damages of 40 reference units.

## §2 — Order after the conciliation hearing (issued 2023-05-04)
At the conciliation hearing set for this date the Respondent, Robin Marsh, failed to
appear in person. The Respondent had filed a medical certificate stating she was
unfit for work during the period. The Tribunal, applying Rule 20, hereby **declares
the Respondent in DEFAULT**, and her defense is consequently precluded. Personal
attendance being mandatory, the consequence of the Respondent's absence is default.
*(Signed: Presiding Judge, Chamber 7.)*

## §3 — Order after the instruction hearing (issued 2024-07-15)
At the instruction hearing set for this date the Claimant, Jordan Vale, failed to
appear and offered no justification for the absence. Rule 51(I) provides that the
action is extinguished where the claimant fails to appear at the hearing without
justification. Accordingly the Tribunal **DISMISSES the action without resolution of
the merits**, under Rule 51(I), and orders the Claimant to bear the costs.
*(Signed: Presiding Judge, Chamber 7.)*

## §4 — Judgment on the merits (issued 2025-03-20)
The Tribunal, having considered the record, finds the claim **PARTLY FOUNDED**. The
Respondent, Robin Marsh, is **condemned to pay** moral damages of 18 reference units,
with interest from the date of the harmful event. The Tribunal further finds the
Respondent liable for **bad-faith litigation** and imposes a penalty of 5% of the
case value for having altered the truth of the facts and resisted the proceeding.
*(Signed: Presiding Judge, Chamber 7.)*

## §5 — Respondent's appeal brief (filed 2025-04-08)
Counsel for the Respondent submits that the judgment cannot stand. The Tribunal
**contradicted itself** and **treated the parties unequally**: it punished the
Respondent's medically-justified absence with default, yet excused the Claimant's
unjustified absence; and it condemned the Respondent on the merits after the action
had already been dismissed. These are grave errors that void the judgment.
